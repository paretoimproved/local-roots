# Local-Roots: Go-to-Market Strategy

## Executive Summary

Local-Roots enters a direct-to-consumer farm sales market that reached $17.5 billion in 2022 (USDA Census of Agriculture) and continues growing at 4.6% annually in real terms. The farm subscription box segment alone is valued at $8.2 billion in 2025 with 19% annual growth. Our strategy is to win farmers first through zero-cost, zero-friction onboarding, then leverage each farmer's existing customer relationships to pull buyers onto the platform organically. Geographic focus is hyperlocal: one metro area at a time, reaching critical density before expanding.

---

## 1. Positioning & Messaging

### Brand Positioning

**Position:** The simplest way for small farmers to turn walk-up customers into recurring subscribers.

Local-Roots is not another farm e-commerce platform. Competitors like Barn2Door ($99-$299/month + $399-$599 setup), Local Line ($49+/month), GrazeCart ($124+/month), and Harvie ($500 setup + 7% of sales + 3% processing) all charge farmers significant monthly fees or take large revenue cuts. Local-Roots charges farmers nothing. The 5% buyer service fee means farmers keep 100% of their listed price, and they never see an invoice from us.

**Positioning statement:** "For small and mid-size farmers who already sell direct to customers, Local-Roots is the subscription tool that turns one-time buyers into weekly regulars, without monthly fees, setup costs, or complicated software."

### Tagline Evaluation

**Current tagline:** "Seasonal food, sold by neighbors."

**Assessment:** This tagline is warm and community-oriented, but it speaks primarily to buyers. It does not communicate the core value proposition to farmers (the side we need to win first), nor does it convey the subscription/convenience angle that differentiates Local-Roots.

**Recommended alternatives to test:**

| Tagline | Audience | Strength |
|---------|----------|----------|
| "Seasonal food, sold by neighbors." (current) | Buyer-facing | Evokes community, trust |
| "Your farm stand, always open." | Farmer-facing | Speaks to the subscription value |
| "Subscribe to your farmer." | Dual | Simple, clear, action-oriented |
| "Know your farmer. Never miss a harvest." | Buyer-facing | Trust + subscription angle |

**Recommendation:** Use "Subscribe to your farmer." as the primary tagline. It works for both sides: farmers hear "people subscribe to you" and buyers hear "get food from a real farmer on repeat." Keep "Seasonal food, sold by neighbors" as secondary copy for buyer-facing landing pages.

### Farmer-Facing Messaging

Farmers are skeptical of technology platforms. They have been burned by services that charge monthly fees, take percentage cuts, or require hours of setup. Messaging must address these objections head-on.

**Core farmer messages:**

1. **"Free to use. No monthly fees. No setup costs."** Lead with this. It is the single biggest differentiator.
2. **"Your customers scan, subscribe, and pick up. You just pack the box."** Emphasize that Local-Roots reduces work, not adds it.
3. **"Turn walk-up buyers into weekly regulars."** This is the outcome farmers care about: predictable revenue.
4. **"You set the price, the box contents, and the pickup schedule. We handle payments."** Farmers stay in control.
5. **"Already have customers? Print a QR code poster and they can subscribe in 60 seconds."** The QR code feature is the hook.

**Messaging tone:** Practical, direct, no-nonsense. Avoid startup jargon. Speak like a neighbor, not a salesperson.

### Buyer-Facing Messaging

**Core buyer messages:**

1. **"Fresh, seasonal food from farms near you, delivered to a pickup spot on your schedule."**
2. **"Subscribe once, pick up weekly. No apps to check, no orders to place."**
3. **"See exactly what is in your box before pickup. Swap items if you want."** (if applicable)
4. **"5% service fee. That is it. Your farmer gets every dollar of the listed price."**
5. **"Support real farms in your community, not a warehouse in another state."**

## 2. Go-to-Market Strategy

### Which Side First: Farmers

Local-Roots must acquire the supply side (farmers) before the demand side (buyers). This is the correct sequencing for three reasons:

1. **Farmers are the harder side.** They are more skeptical, slower to adopt, and have fewer alternatives pulling them toward platforms. Once farmers are onboarded, buyers are 2-10x easier to acquire (NFX marketplace research).
2. **Farmers bring their own customers.** A farmer with an existing farm stand, CSA, or farmers market booth has 50-500 existing customers. The QR code poster converts those relationships into platform users at near-zero CAC.
3. **Empty demand is worse than empty supply.** A buyer who signs up and sees no farms nearby will never return. But a farmer who signs up and has no platform buyers yet can still use the QR code to convert their own walk-up customers.

### Farmer Acquisition Playbook

**Phase 1: Direct outreach (Weeks 1-4)**

Target: 10 farmers in a single metro area.

| Step | Action | Details |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1 | Build target list | Visit local farmers markets in person. Identify 30-50 vendors who sell produce, meat, eggs, or specialty items. Prioritize those who already run informal CSAs or take pre-orders. |
| 2 | In-person pitch | Show up at their stand. Buy something. Then pitch: "I built a free tool that lets your customers subscribe to a weekly box. You set the price. Here is a QR code poster, that is all you need." |
| 3 | Demo with their phone | Show them the farmer setup flow on their own phone. If it takes more than 5 minutes, the product needs more work before launch. |
| 4 | Leave a QR code poster | Print a physical poster they can put up at their stand that day. First subscriber should come within the week. |
| 5 | Weekly check-in | Call or text every farmer weekly for the first month. Fix issues immediately. |

**Phase 2: Referral and word-of-mouth (Months 2-3)**

- Ask every onboarded farmer: "Do you know another farmer who would want this?" Farmers talk to each other at markets, through co-ops, and at extension events.
- Offer a "founding farmer" badge or listing priority for early adopters.
- Attend one agricultural extension or cooperative meeting per week to present.

**Phase 3: Scaled outreach (Months 4-6)**

- Partner with farmers market organizations to present at vendor meetings.
- Run a "sign up in 5 minutes" challenge at farm conferences and ag expos.
- Create a 2-minute farmer testimonial video and distribute through ag newsletters.

### Buyer Acquisition Playbook

**Phase 0: Farmer-led acquisition (the primary engine)**

This is the most important channel: each farmer converts their own existing customers via the QR code poster. If a farmer has 200 weekly farm stand visitors and 5% scan and subscribe, that is 10 subscribers per farmer per week with zero platform CAC. With 10 farmers, that is 100 subscribers in the first month.

**Phase 1: Local organic (Months 1-3)**

- Optimize for local SEO: "farm subscription box [city]", "CSA near me", "local farm pickup [neighborhood]". Farmers markets with active digital presences see up to 30% higher traffic (Cornell University).
- Create a farm directory page for each metro area. Each farm gets its own SEO-optimized profile page.
- Post on local Facebook groups, Nextdoor, and community boards. These are high-trust, low-cost channels for local food buyers.

**Phase 2: Content and community (Months 3-6)**

- Seasonal recipe content tied to what is in the box this week.
- "Meet your farmer" profile series on social media.
- Partner with local food bloggers and micro-influencers (2K-50K followers). Micro-influencer partnerships drive 23% higher conversion rates than standard ads.

**Phase 3: Paid and partnerships (Months 6+)**

- Hyper-targeted Facebook/Instagram ads within a 15-mile radius of active pickup locations.
- Partner with local gyms, yoga studios, and wellness businesses for cross-promotion.
- Co-marketing with complementary local food businesses (bakeries, coffee roasters).

### Geographic Strategy: Hyperlocal First

**Launch in a single metro area.** Do not spread across multiple cities until you achieve:

- 10+ active farmers in the metro area
- 200+ active subscribers
- 80%+ weekly pickup rate

**Metro selection criteria:**

| Factor | Why It Matters |
|--------|---------------|
| Active farmers market scene | Indicates existing D2C farmer supply |
| Educated, health-conscious population | Higher willingness to pay for local food |
| Existing CSA culture | Buyers already understand the model |
| Manageable geography | Pickup locations within 20-minute drive for most buyers |
| Limited existing competition | No dominant local CSA platform |

**Selected first market: Eugene, OR.** Eugene was chosen for the following reasons:

- **Right size:** ~175K population — large enough to find engaged food buyers, small enough to achieve network density quickly.
- **Strong local food culture:** Eugene has a deep farm-to-table tradition, significant CSA adoption, and a health-conscious, university-influenced population (University of Oregon).
- **Rich farm supply:** Lane County hosts 75+ farms, 24+ CSAs, and the Lane County Farmers Market (100+ vendors, operating 11 months/year). The Willamette Valley is one of the most productive agricultural regions in the Pacific Northwest.
- **Limited platform competition:** No dominant local CSA platform operating in the market.
- **Founder proximity:** Close enough for the in-person, relationship-first launch strategy the playbook requires.

**Expansion pattern:** Concentric. After proving the model in Eugene, expand to 2-3 similar Oregon/Pacific Northwest cities (e.g., Corvallis, Bend, Salem). State-level expansion enables shared relationships with Oregon State Extension Service and Oregon cooperative networks. Comparable second-market candidates nationwide: Asheville NC, Burlington VT, Ann Arbor MI, Madison WI, Boise ID.

## 3. Channel Strategy

### Customer Acquisition Cost Targets

General CAC benchmarks for 2025-2026 show costs rising 40-60% industry-wide. For Local-Roots, the goal is to keep buyer CAC under $10 by leveraging the farmer-led QR code channel as the primary acquisition engine.

| Channel | Side | Estimated CAC | Priority |
|---------|------|---------------|----------|
| QR code poster (farmer-led) | Buyer | $0.50-$2.00 | Primary |
| Founder direct outreach | Farmer | $50-$100 (time cost) | Primary |
| Local SEO / organic search | Buyer | $5-$15 | High |
| Social media (organic) | Both | $3-$8 | High |
| Farmer referral | Farmer | $10-$20 | High |
| Local community groups (Nextdoor, FB) | Buyer | $2-$5 | High |
| Buyer referral program | Buyer | $8-$12 | Medium |
| Partnerships (ag extensions, co-ops) | Farmer | $15-$30 | Medium |
| Micro-influencer partnerships | Buyer | $10-$20 | Medium |
| Paid social (hyper-targeted) | Buyer | $15-$30 | Low (later) |
| Paid search | Buyer | $25-$50 | Low (later) |

### Organic Channels (Lowest CAC, Highest Priority)

**SEO and content:**
- Build individual farm profile pages optimized for "[farm name] subscription" and "farm box [city]".
- Create a blog with seasonal content: "What is in season in [city] this week", recipes, and farmer stories.
- Target long-tail keywords: "weekly farm box [city]", "local produce subscription [state]", "CSA alternative [city]".
- Claim and optimize Google Business Profiles for each pickup location.

**Social media:**
- Instagram and TikTok for buyer acquisition. Short-form video of box reveals, farm visits, and seasonal harvests.
- Facebook for community building. Local groups are the highest-trust channel for food recommendations.
- Keep posting cadence at 3-5x/week. Prioritize authenticity over polish.

**Email marketing:**
- Collect emails from day one via the QR code flow (email required to subscribe).
- Weekly "What is in your box" emails drive engagement and reduce churn.
- Seasonal re-engagement campaigns for lapsed subscribers.

### Partnership Channels

**Agricultural extension services:**
- The Cooperative Extension System has offices in nearly all 3,000 U.S. counties and actively promotes technology adoption for farmers.
- Offer to present at extension workshops, field days, and small farm conferences.
- Position Local-Roots as a tool that aligns with extension goals: helping small farms increase revenue through direct sales.

**Farmers market organizations:**
- Many cities have farmers market associations or management organizations.
- Offer to be a technology partner. Local-Roots makes their vendor farmers more successful, which makes the market stronger.
- Co-brand QR code posters with the market's logo.

**Food co-ops and buying clubs:**
- Natural channel partners. Co-ops already aggregate local food buyers.
- Offer co-ops a custom landing page or affiliate link.

**Farm-to-school and farm-to-institution programs:**
- Longer sales cycle but high volume. A school district subscribing to 5 local farms is a significant anchor.

### Referral and Viral Loops

**Buyer referral program:**
- "Give $5, get $5" credit on the service fee. Simple, proven.
- Trigger referral prompt after the third successful pickup (not the first, to ensure quality referrals).
- Make sharing easy: one-tap share to text message with a personalized link.

**Farmer referral program:**
- "Your farmer friend signs up, you both get featured in our weekly newsletter."
- Non-monetary incentives work better for farmers. They care about exposure and credibility, not $10 credits.

**Built-in viral loop (the QR code poster):**
- This is the primary viral mechanic. Every QR code poster at a farm stand is a physical advertisement for Local-Roots that generates scans from walk-up customers.
- The scan-to-subscribe flow should include a "Tell a friend" prompt on the confirmation page.
- Consider adding the Local-Roots URL or a second QR code on the physical box/bag that subscribers pick up, turning every box into a walking advertisement.

## 4. Pricing & Revenue Model

### Current Model Assessment: 5% Buyer Service Fee

**The 5% buyer-only fee is a strong strategic choice.** Here is why:

**Competitive comparison:**

| Platform | Farmer Pays | Buyer Pays | Setup Fee |
|----------|-------------|------------|-----------|
| **Local-Roots** | **$0** | **5% service fee** | **$0** |
| Barn2Door | $99-$299/month + 2.9% + $0.30 per txn | $0 | $399-$599 |
| Local Line | $49+/month | $0 | $0 |
| GrazeCart | $124+/month | $0 | $0 |
| Harvie | 7% of sales + 3% processing | Delivery fee ($6) | $500 |
| Instacart | Commission (varies) | Service + delivery fees | $0 |

**Key insight:** Every competitor charges the farmer. Local-Roots is the only platform where farmers pay nothing. This is the single most powerful farmer acquisition message.

### Is 5% the Right Number?

**For buyers:** A 5% fee on a $40 weekly box is $2.00/week. This is:
- Less than a single item delivery fee on Instacart
- Less than the Harvie delivery fee ($6)
- Comparable to a tip at a coffee shop
- Well below the 15-30% service fees charged by DoorDash, Uber Eats, etc.

Most buyers will find $2/week acceptable for the convenience of a managed subscription.

**For platform economics:** At $40 average box price and 5% fee, Local-Roots earns $2.00 per box per week. With 52 weeks and reasonable retention:

| Metric | Conservative | Moderate | Optimistic |
|--------|-------------|----------|-----------|
| Avg box price | $35 | $40 | $50 |
| Service fee (5%) | $1.75 | $2.00 | $2.50 |
| Annual weeks active | 40 | 44 | 48 |
| Revenue per subscriber/year | $70 | $88 | $120 |
| 1,000 subscribers | $70K | $88K | $120K |
| 5,000 subscribers | $350K | $440K | $600K |

**Recommendation:** Keep the 5% buyer fee. Do not add seller fees. The zero-cost-to-farmer model is the core competitive advantage and the foundation of the farmer acquisition strategy. Introducing seller fees, even small ones, would destroy the primary selling point.

### Payment Processing

The 5% service fee must cover payment processing costs. Standard payment processing (Stripe) is 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction.

On a $40 box: Processing cost = $1.46. Service fee revenue = $2.00. Net margin = $0.54 per transaction (27% of service fee, 1.35% of GMV).

This is thin but workable at scale. Two options to improve unit economics:
1. **ACH payments:** Offer buyers the option to pay via bank transfer at lower processing fees (0.8% via Stripe, capped at $5). On a $40 box, ACH processing = $0.32, leaving $1.68 net margin.
2. **Volume discounts from Stripe:** At scale, negotiate custom rates below 2.9% + $0.30.

### Disintermediation Defense: Deposit / No-Show Fee Split

The biggest pricing risk is buyers paying farmers directly (cash/Venmo) to avoid the 5% fee. Since every transaction involves an in-person pickup, this is structurally possible. The primary defense: embed the platform in the financial accountability layer, not just the convenience layer.

**Proposed model:** Every subscription holds a refundable deposit ($5-10) on the buyer's card. On successful pickup, the deposit is released. On no-show, the deposit is split between the farmer (compensation for wasted produce) and the platform (enforcement revenue). This makes the farmer an active advocate for keeping buyers on-platform -- off-platform buyers have no deposit, no accountability, and cost the farmer money when they flake. See `04-ceo-strategy-memo.md` Risk 6 for full analysis.

### Future Revenue Opportunities (Do Not Launch With These)

- **Premium farmer tools:** Analytics dashboard, CRM features, marketing tools. Offer free basic tier, charge for premium. Only after establishing a large free user base.
- **Sponsored placement:** Larger farms pay for featured listing in buyer discovery. Only after organic discovery is working.
- **Marketplace advertising:** Partner brands (seed companies, farm supply) advertise to the farmer audience. Only at significant scale.
- **White-label or API:** Larger farms or food hubs pay for white-label subscription infrastructure. Only if demand emerges.

## 5. Launch Playbook

### Pre-Launch (Weeks 1-2)

| Day | Action |
|-----|--------|
| 1-3 | Launch metro: Eugene, OR. Lane County Farmers Market (100+ vendors, 11 months/year) is the primary target venue. Confirm additional markets in the area (Saturday Market, producer-only markets). |
| 3-5 | Visit all target farmers markets. Buy from vendors. Take notes on who has existing pre-order or CSA programs, who has the biggest crowds, who is most tech-comfortable. The Willamette Valley farm community is tight-knit — early relationships compound quickly. |
| 5-7 | Build a list of 30-50 target farmers with names, what they sell, and contact info (often on market websites or farm social media). |
| 7-10 | Prepare pitch materials: one-page handout, QR code poster template, 60-second elevator pitch script. |
| 10-14 | Set up landing pages: farmer signup page, buyer-facing farm directory for the metro area. Ensure the farmer onboarding flow takes under 5 minutes. |

### Launch: First 10 Farmers (Weeks 3-6)

| Week | Goal | How |
|------|------|-----|
| 3 | 3 farmers signed up | Visit farmers markets Saturday morning. Pitch in person. Offer to set up their store and print their QR poster on the spot. Target the 3 most receptive vendors from scouting. |
| 4 | 5 farmers signed up | Follow up with interested vendors from Week 3. Ask first 3 farmers for introductions to other vendors. Attend one weekday farmers market. |
| 5 | 8 farmers signed up | Reach out to farms not at markets: roadside stands, u-pick operations, farm stores. Check local farm directories (LocalHarvest, state ag department listings). |
| 6 | 10 farmers signed up | Contact local agricultural extension office. Ask to present at their next small farm workshop. Email remaining prospects with testimonial from first farmers. |

**Success criteria for a "signed up" farmer:**
- Profile created with at least one subscription box listed
- Price and pickup schedule set
- QR code poster printed and displayed at their selling location

### Launch: First 100 Buyers (Weeks 3-8)

| Source | Expected Buyers | How |
|--------|----------------|-----|
| QR code scans at farm stands | 40-60 | Each of 10 farmers has their poster displayed. At 5% scan-to-subscribe rate on ~100 weekly visitors = ~5 subscribers per farmer over 4 weeks. |
| Farmer's existing email list / social media | 20-30 | Ask each farmer to send one email or post one social media update announcing their Local-Roots subscription. Provide them with copy and images. |
| Founder's personal network | 10-15 | Friends, family, local contacts. Seed the platform with real users. |
| Local community groups (Nextdoor, Facebook) | 10-20 | Post in local food/farming groups. Not as an ad, but as "hey, I built this thing for our local farmers." |
| Local press / blog coverage | 5-10 | Pitch one local news outlet or food blog. "Local founder builds free tool for farmers" is a good local interest story. |

### Post-Launch: Weeks 8-12

| Priority | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Retention | Call every farmer weekly. Fix any issues same-day. Track pickup rates. If a farmer's subscribers are not picking up, work with them on box contents and communication. |
| Measurement | Track: subscriber growth rate, weekly pickup rate, farmer satisfaction (NPS), buyer satisfaction, churn rate, QR scan-to-subscribe conversion rate. |
| Iteration | Based on data, refine: onboarding flow, box management tools, pickup reminders, referral program mechanics. |
| Expansion prep | If metrics are healthy (80%+ pickup rate, <10% monthly churn, positive farmer NPS), begin scouting second metro area. |

### Key Milestones

| Milestone | Target Date | Metric |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| First farmer onboarded | Week 3 | 1 farmer with live subscription box |
| First buyer subscription | Week 3-4 | 1 paying subscriber |
| 10 farmers | Week 6 | 10 active farmers with QR posters |
| 100 subscribers | Week 8 | 100 active subscribers |
| First month retention | Week 12 | >85% of Month 1 subscribers still active |
| $1,000 monthly GMV | Week 8-10 | ~25 subscribers at $40/week |
| $10,000 monthly GMV | Month 4-5 | ~250 subscribers at $40/week |

## 6. Content & Community Strategy

### Building Trust with the Farming Community

Farmers are the most important audience and the hardest to win. Trust is built through actions, not marketing.

**Principles:**

1. **Show up in person.** The founder must be physically present at farmers markets, farm events, and extension meetings. Digital outreach alone will not work. Farmers trust people they have met face-to-face.

2. **Lead with service, not sales.** Help farmers with their existing challenges first. Offer to photograph their products, help them set up an Instagram page, or connect them with a restaurant buyer. Build goodwill before pitching.

3. **Never overpromise.** Do not tell farmers "you will get 50 new subscribers." Say "let us put up a poster and see what happens." Under-promise, over-deliver.

4. **Be transparent about the business model.** Farmers will ask "how do you make money?" Answer clearly: "We charge buyers a 5% service fee. You pay nothing. If we do not deliver value, we do not make money."

5. **Feature farmers, not the platform.** All content should center the farmer's story, not Local-Roots. We are the infrastructure, they are the stars.

### Content Calendar (First 3 Months)

**Blog (1 post/week):**
- Week 1: "Why we built Local-Roots" (founder story)
- Week 2: Farm profile #1: "[Farmer name]'s story"
- Week 3: "What is in season in [city] this month"
- Week 4: "How to get the most out of your farm box" (buyer tips)
- Repeat pattern: founder/mission, farmer profile, seasonal guide, buyer education

**Social media (3-5 posts/week across Instagram, Facebook, TikTok):**
- Monday: What is in the box this week (photo or short video)
- Wednesday: Farmer spotlight or behind-the-scenes farm content
- Friday: Recipe or meal prep idea using box contents
- Saturday: Farmers market content (live stories, vendor features)
- Sunday: Community content (subscriber photos, testimonials)

**Email (1/week to subscribers):**
- What is in your box this week
- Farmer of the week
- One recipe
- Pickup reminder

### Community Building Tactics

**For farmers:**
- Create a private group (Facebook group or group text) for Local-Roots farmers in each metro area. Let them share tips, coordinate, and feel part of something.
- Host a quarterly farmer dinner (Local-Roots buys the meal). No pitch, just relationship building.
- Share platform data transparently: how many subscribers, growth trends, what buyers are searching for.

**For buyers:**
- Encourage subscribers to share box photos on social media with a consistent hashtag.
- Run seasonal challenges: "Cook everything in your box this week" photo contest.
- Host one farm visit per quarter where subscribers can meet their farmer. These events are high-engagement and generate excellent organic content.

**For the broader community:**
- Partner with local food banks: for every 100 subscriptions, donate 5 boxes to families in need. This generates goodwill and press coverage.
- Sponsor a booth at community events (Earth Day, local food festivals).
- Collaborate with schools on farm education programs. Position Local-Roots as a community asset, not just a business.

## 7. Risk Mitigation

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Farmers do not adopt (too tech-averse) | Medium | Critical | Offer white-glove onboarding. Founder sets up the store for them. QR poster is a physical object, not an app to learn. |
| Buyers do not retain (high churn) | Medium | High | Subscription box churn averages 10-12% monthly. Combat with: weekly "what is in your box" emails, flexible skip/pause, and excellent box quality. Target <8% monthly churn. |
| Seasonal drop-off | High | Medium | Many farms are seasonal. Plan for winter lull. Promote farms with year-round products (meat, eggs, dairy, preserved goods). Run re-engagement campaigns in spring. |
| Competitor copies the free model | Low | Medium | First-mover advantage in each metro area. Network effects (more farmers = more buyers = more farmers) create a moat. Relationships with farmers are hard to replicate. |
| Unit economics too thin at 5% | Medium | High | Monitor closely. If needed, introduce optional premium buyer features (priority pickup, box customization) or premium farmer tools. Do not add seller fees. |
